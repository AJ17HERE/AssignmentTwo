Chola bronze is a form of metal sculpture that originated in the southern Indian state of Tamil Nadu during the Chola dynasty, which ruled from the 9th to the 13th century. Chola bronze sculptures are known for their intricate details, lifelike proportions, and intricate ornamentation. Chola bronze sculptures were primarily created using the lost-wax casting technique, which involves creating a wax model of the sculpture and then covering it with layers of clay, which is then heated and filled with molten metal. The sculptures were often made of bronze, an alloy of copper and tin, and were coated with gold leaf or painted with bright colors. The Chola bronze sculptures were created primarily for religious purposes and depict Hindu deities such as Shiva, Vishnu, and Devi, as well as other figures from Hindu mythology. The sculptures were often placed in temples and used in religious ceremonies. Chola bronze sculptures are considered to be some of the finest examples of Indian art, and many are now housed in museums and private collections around the world. The art form has influenced modern Indian sculpture and has also been used as inspiration for contemporary artists.
