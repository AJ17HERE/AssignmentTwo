Tanjore painting is a traditional art form that originated in the town of Thanjavur (Tanjore) in the southern Indian state of Tamil Nadu. This art form is known for its intricate details, vibrant colors, and use of gold leaf and semi-precious stones. Tanjore paintings are characterized by the use of rich, vivid colors, often depicting Hindu gods and goddesses, mythological scenes, and royalty. The paintings are done on a wooden plank and have a distinct three-dimensional effect. The surface is first coated with a mixture of limestone powder and adhesive, and the painting is done using natural dyes, semi-precious stones, and gold leaf. The art form is believed to have originated during the 16th century under the patronage of the Nayakas of Thanjavur. Over time, the art form evolved, with influences from the Maratha, Vijayanagara, and Mughal empires. Today, Tanjore painting is still popular in southern India and is used in the creation of wall hangings, jewelry boxes, and other decorative items. The art form has also been recognized as a Geographical Indication (GI) product by the Indian government, which aims to protect and promote the traditional art form.
